PET vs PP Food Containers: Which Material is Right for Your Application?

Two plastics dominate the food service container market: PET (polyethylene terephthalate) and PP (polypropylene). Both are FDA-approved for food contact, both are widely recyclable, and both are cost-effective at scale. But they behave very differently in real-world food service environments — and choosing the wrong one can hurt your product presentation, customer satisfaction, and sustainability claims.

Clarity: PET Wins Clearly

PET achieves optical clarity virtually indistinguishable from glass. Its refractive index creates crystal-clear visibility that is essential for grab-and-go merchandising, fresh food display, and any application where the product sells itself at point of purchase.

PP is naturally translucent — not clear. While some thin-gauge PP products achieve decent see-through quality, they cannot match PET for premium food presentation. If your application requires consumers to clearly see the product before purchasing, PET is the only choice.

Temperature Resistance: PP Wins for Heat

This is PP's clear advantage. Polypropylene has a melt point around 160°C, making it microwave-safe and suitable for hot-fill applications up to ~120°C. It won't warp or deform in warming display cases or when used for hot grab-and-go items like soups, entrees, or steam-table sides.

PET begins to soften at around 70—80°C. Standard PET containers are not microwave-safe and can warp or deform if exposed to hot liquids or direct heat. Crystallized PET (CPET) extends heat resistance significantly — but adds cost and reduces clarity.

Cold Performance: Both Work Well

Both PET and PP maintain good structural integrity in refrigerated and frozen environments. PET containers can safely handle temperatures down to -20°C without becoming brittle. PP has similar cold performance. Neither material is a winner here — both are suitable for refrigerated display and cold chain transport.

Recyclability: PET Has Better Infrastructure

PET (#1) has a well-established collection and recycling infrastructure across North America. It is one of the most widely accepted materials in curbside programs and can be recycled into fibers, new containers, and packaging film. rPET — recycled PET — is commercially available at scale for food-contact reuse.

PP (#5) is recycled in fewer programs, particularly at the curbside level. While PP recycling capacity is growing, the end markets and collection rates significantly lag PET. If sustainability claims and recycled content are a priority for your brand or retail buyer, PET has a structural advantage.

Cost

Raw material costs fluctuate with crude oil prices, but PET and PP are generally within 5—15% of each other on a per-pound basis. PP is typically slightly less expensive, but the difference narrows considerably when comparing fully finished, food-contact grade products at similar gauge weights. Tooling costs are comparable for both materials.
