How Thermoformed Packaging is Made — Behind the Manufacturing Process

Most food service buyers interact with thermoformed containers every day without knowing how they're made. Understanding the manufacturing process helps procurement teams ask better questions, set realistic tolerances, and build more productive supplier relationships.

Step 1: Resin Selection and Blending

The process begins with raw plastic resin in pellet form. For our rPET products, virgin PET resin is blended with a defined percentage of post-consumer recycled PET — typically 30% or higher. The blending ratio must be precisely controlled because recycled content affects clarity, melt viscosity, and mechanical properties. All resins used in food-contact products must comply with FDA 21 CFR standards.

Step 2: Sheet Extrusion

Resin pellets are fed into a large extruder where they are melted, mixed, and forced through a flat die to produce a continuous plastic sheet. The extruder temperature profile and line speed control the sheet's gauge (thickness), clarity, and surface finish. Consistent sheet gauge is critical — variations of even 0.002" can affect wall strength, stack height, and lid sealing performance in the finished container.

Step 3: Thermoforming

The extruded sheet is fed into a thermoforming press, where it is heated to a precise forming temperature — for PET, typically 140—160°C. Once at forming temperature, the sheet is draped over or pressed into a metal mold using vacuum, pressure, or a combination of both. The mold defines the container's final geometry: walls, base, rim profile, and any textural features.

At Eatery Essentials, our Dallas, TX facility operates multiple high-speed thermoforming lines capable of producing thousands of units per hour per tool cavity — enabling the scale required to supply major retail and food service accounts.

Step 4: Trimming and Punching

After forming, the continuous sheet containing multiple formed containers passes through a trim press that punches out individual units. The remaining plastic "web" is ground and can be re-introduced into the extrusion process at controlled levels — another element of the circular manufacturing approach.

Step 5: Quality Inspection

Finished containers pass through automated vision systems that check for dimensional compliance, wall defects, clarity consistency, and lip/rim integrity. Containers failing inspection criteria are rejected and reground. Statistical sampling validates AQL levels for every production run, and records are maintained for traceability.

Step 6: Stacking, Packaging, and Shipment

Approved containers are automatically counted, stacked, sleeved or bagged, packed into master cases, and palletized. Our Dallas facility stocks finished goods across 2 million square feet of combined space, enabling same-week fulfillment for the vast majority of our product catalog.
